# The best highlighter to actually buy 🖍️

> A bit of a side quest. I built a library that fakes highlighter ink in the browser, and somewhere along the way I ended up reading far too much about the real ones. So here's what I'd actually buy, by use case. For the physics behind all of it, see [how-highlighters-work.md](./how-highlighters-work.md).

There's really one rule, and everything here follows from it: **a good highlighter makes the text easier to read, not harder.** The bad ones go on too dark, too opaque, or they drag the ink underneath into a smear. That's the whole game.

## If you only buy one

Get a [**Zebra Mildliner**](https://www.zebrapen.com/products/mildliner-double-ended-highlighter). The colours are muted, so they sit behind your words instead of shouting over them; the twin tip gives you a broad end for highlighting and a fine end for notes; and the range is big enough to colour-code a whole textbook without any two shades fighting. It's the one I reach for without thinking.

The one catch: it smears fresh fountain-pen and gel ink. Over printed pages or ballpoint it's perfect. If you write with a fountain pen, jump to the gel/dry section below.

## By use case

### Studying and colour-coding
The [Mildliner](https://www.zebrapen.com/products/mildliner-double-ended-highlighter), again. The muted shades are the point: you can run five colours down a page and it still looks calm, and the fine tip means you don't need a second pen for margin notes. If your colour system matters to you, this is the one. Just keep it off wet fountain-pen ink.

### Precision (textbooks, tight margins)
The [Uni Propus Window](https://www.mp-uni.com/th/en/product/propus-window/) fixes the single most annoying thing about highlighters: the tip hides the exact text you're trying to stop at, so you always overshoot into the margin. The transparent window lets you see straight through it. The ink dries fast too, so lefties don't drag it.

### Planners, and anything you might redo
[Pilot FriXion Light](https://www.pilot-frixion.uk/uk/frixion-family/frixion-softslight/). The ink disappears under friction heat, so you can re-highlight as much as you like with no eraser mess. The trade-off is real, though: anything over about 60°C (a hot car, a laminator) wipes it blank, and the freezer brings it back. Never use it on something permanent or legal.

### Bible, dictionary, very thin paper
Reach for a **gel or dry highlighter**: [Sharpie Gel](https://www.sharpie.com/highlighters/sharpie-gel-highlighters-bullet-tip/SP_1184439.html), a "Bible" gel highlighter from Pentel, or a twist-up wax stick. They lay down almost no liquid, so they don't bleed through tissue-thin pages or reactivate the printed ink. One honest caveat: bleed is partly a property of the paper, so even a "no-bleed" pen can ghost on the very thinnest stock. Test a corner first.

### Highlighting over your own pen ink
Skip wet dye highlighters here. Use a **gel** or **dry** highlighter, or let your ink dry fully and reach for a smear-resistant wet one like the [Staedtler Textsurfer Classic](https://www.staedtler.com/intl/en/products/markers/highlighters/textsurfer-classic-364-c-highlighter-364-cwp4/). Better still: if you know you'll highlight, write with ballpoint in the first place.

### Journaling and the pretty stuff
The [Mildliner](https://www.zebrapen.com/products/mildliner-double-ended-highlighter) basically started the muted-highlighter look; [Stabilo Boss Pastel](https://www.stabilo.com/com/products/highlighting/pastel-highlighters/stabilo-boss-original-pastel/) has the widest pastel range. Want shimmer? [Sun-Star LAMECO](https://www.jetpens.com/Sun-Star-LAMECO-Clear-Glitter-Marker-3-Color-Set/pd/50361) glitter markers are clear, so they add sparkle over writing without tinting it.

### Refillable and low-waste
[Stabilo Green Boss](https://www.stabilo.com/com/products/highlighting/highlighters/stabilo-green-boss/) (83% recycled, refillable) or [Schneider Job](https://schneiderpen.com/us/highlighter/job/1504) (refillable, and the first highlighter to earn the Blue Angel eco-label). A tight cap matters more than you'd think: most highlighters die from drying out long before the ink runs low.

### Cheap and everywhere
[Sharpie](https://www.sharpie.com/highlighters/) or any store-brand fluorescent is genuinely fine for printed handouts. Don't expect them to behave on thin paper, and expect louder colours.

## The one distinction worth knowing: dye vs pigment

- **Dye-based** (most cheap fluorescents, Tombow Play Color): brighter and more saturated, but it fades over time and isn't water-resistant. Great for notes you'll throw away.
- **Pigment-based** ([Mildliner](https://www.zebrapen.com/products/mildliner-double-ended-highlighter), [Tombow Kei Coat](https://www.tombow.com/en/products/kei_coat/), [Sakura Pigma](https://www.sakuraofamerica.com/product/pigma-micron/)): more muted and translucent, but archival, water-resistant once dry, and it layers cleanly without smearing. Great for anything you'll keep.

If you remember nothing else, remember this axis. It quietly predicts brightness, fade, smear, and bleed all at once.

## The usual complaints, and how to dodge them

- **Smears the ink underneath** → gel or dry highlighter, or ballpoint plus drying time.
- **Bleeds through** → thicker, well-sized paper; gel or dry highlighter.
- **Dries out** → it's almost always the cap. Close it firmly, or buy anti-dry-out / refillable.
- **Colours too harsh** → this is exactly why pastel and mild lines exist.
- **Streaky, frayed tip** → cheap fibre nibs. Bonded/sintered nibs on the premium pens hold their edge.
