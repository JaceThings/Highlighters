# The Best Highlighter to Actually Buy 🖍️

> A sidequest. We built a whole library simulating highlighters, so we did the research on *real* ones too. This is the "which one do I buy" guide - opinionated, by use case. For the physics behind all of it, see **[how-highlighters-work.md](./how-highlighters-work.md)**.

The single rule that decides everything: **a great highlighter makes text *more* readable, not less.** Mediocre ones obscure text (too dark/opaque) or smear the ink underneath. Every recommendation below is a variation on that.

## By use case, with reasoning

### 📚 Studying / note-taking / color-coding
**Zebra Mildliner.** This is the one most people should buy. The muted "mild" colors don't scream over your text, the palette is curated into harmonious mood sets (so 5 colors actually look good together), and the twin-tip gives you a broad highlight end and a fine end for annotations in one pen. **Caveat:** it smears fountain-pen and gel ink - pair it with ballpoint or printed/laser pages. If you write with a fountain pen, reach for a gel or dry highlighter instead.

### 🎯 Precision (textbooks, tight layouts)
**Uni Propus Window.** The transparent notch in the chisel tip solves the #1 highlighter annoyance: the tip normally hides the very text you're trying to stop at, so you overshoot. With the window you see through it and stop clean. Quick-dry ink, so left-handers don't smear it.

### ✏️ Planners, mistakes, anything you might redo
**Pilot FriXion Light.** Erasable via friction heat - no eraser crumbs, re-highlight as much as you want. Soft colors keep text readable. **The gotcha:** above ~60 °C the ink vanishes (a hot car, a laminator), and it comes back in the freezer. Don't use it on anything permanent or legal.

### 📖 Bible / dictionary / very thin paper
**A gel or dry highlighter** - Pilot FriXion gel, Sharpie Gel, Pentel's gel/Bible highlighters, or a twist-up **wax "dry highlighter."** These barely deposit liquid, so they don't bleed through tissue-thin pages or reactivate the printed ink. Reality check: bleed is *also* a property of the paper - even "no-bleed" pens can ghost on the thinnest Bible stock, so test a corner first.

### 🖊️ Highlighting over your own pen ink without smearing it
Skip wet dye highlighters. Use a **gel** or **dry** highlighter, or let your ink fully dry and use a **smear-resistant** wet one like the **Staedtler Textsurfer Classic**. Better yet, write with **ballpoint** if you know you'll highlight.

### 🎨 Aesthetic / journaling / studygram
**Mildliner** for the muted palette that started the whole trend; **Stabilo Boss Pastel** for the widest pastel range. Want sparkle? **Sun-Star LAMECO** shimmer markers layer glitter over existing writing.

### 🌱 Sustainability
**Stabilo Green Boss** (83% recycled, refillable, anti-dry-out) or **Schneider Job** (refillable via refill station). Refillable + a tight cap is the eco move - a highlighter dies from a bad cap long before it runs out of ink.

### 💰 Cheap and everywhere
**Sharpie** or store-brand fluorescents are completely fine for printed handouts. Just don't expect them on thin paper, and expect harsher colors.

## The dye vs pigment thing (it explains a lot)

- **Dye-based** (most cheap fluorescents, Tombow Play Color): brighter, more saturated, but **fades** over time and isn't water-resistant. Great for short-lived study notes.
- **Pigment-based** (Mildliner, Tombow Kei Coat, Sakura Pigma): more muted/translucent, **archival**, water-resistant once dry, layers cleanly without smearing. Great for anything you'll keep.

If you only remember one axis, it's this one - it predicts brightness, fade, smear, and bleed all at once.

## What reviewers complain about (so you can avoid it)
- **Smearing the ink underneath** → use gel/dry, or ballpoint + dry time.
- **Bleed-through** → thicker, well-sized paper; gel/dry highlighters.
- **Drying out** → it's almost always the cap; close it firmly, buy anti-dry-out or refillable.
- **Colors too harsh** → that's why pastel/mild exist.
- **Streaky / uneven / frayed tip** → cheap fiber nibs; sintered/bonded nibs (premium) hold up.
